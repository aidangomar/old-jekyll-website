  • An Introduction to the Flying Snake

    I chose the flying snake for my unusual organism project because I have always been dazzled by the way it glides and how it manages to do what other reptiles can't without wings or any other advanced body structures. At first glance you think you are looking at a normal tree snake, but what you're really looking at is an advanced flying machine. Not too much is known about this snake, and it does poorly in capitivity. The hatchlings are around 15-20 centimeters long and can grow up to be four feet in length. The flying snake, scientifically known as the Chrysopelea, is one the most interesting reptilians on the planet. This snake is unique because it can glide from tree to tree. The snake was first discovered in 1826 by the German herpotologist, Heinrich Boie. This snake still exists today, and is part of the Colubrid family which makes up about two-thirds of all current snake species. The Colubrid family has been around since the Paleogene period, and Chrysopelea has been known to science for over 185 years, but nobody knows exactly when or how the flying snake evolved.

  • Adaptations

    The Chrysopelea has many adaptations, but its most well known feature is its ability to glide.
    • Stretchable Ribs: This allows the snake to flatten its body and glide from tree to tree without touching the ground. This also allows them to catch prey in other trees easily. These snakes have been known to glide up to 100 meters (328 feet).
    • Hinged Jaw: This allows them to swallow their prey whole without their jaw getting damaged.
    • Large Eyes: These are particularly helpful for spotting prey from far distances in the rainforest, and allows the snake to calculate where to leap next.
    • Rear Fangs: Using these simple rear fangs the snake can inject its mild venom into its prey without much trouble.

  • Habitat and Diet, and more

      Habitat

      There are several species of Chrysopelea, and all of them live in Eastern Asia. They can be found in rainforests on mainland Southeast Asia, Malaku, Philippines, Idonesia, southernmost China, India, and Sri Lanka. They spend most of their time in the trees and are diurnal (hunt during the day).

      Diet

      Their diet consists mostly of small animals, like lizards, geckos, frogs, birds and bats.
